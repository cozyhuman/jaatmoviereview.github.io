Jaat Movie Review: Brutal, Bloody, and Built for Deol Fans

Gopichand Malineni’s Jaat is a loud, unapologetic action drama that caters primarily to Sunny Deol fans, delivering exactly what it promises: nostalgia, testosterone, and over-the-top heroics. With its Hindi directorial debut, Malineni blends South Indian action flamboyance with North Indian machismo, resulting in a film that’s entertaining, if predictable.

Storyline

Set in a remote coastal village, Ranatunga (Randeep Hooda) rules through fear and oppression. The villagers live under constant torment until a mysterious stranger—Sunny Deol’s Jaat—arrives, uncovers the injustices, and sets out to challenge the tyrant. The narrative revolves around a classic messiah-versus-villain arc, with revenge, loyalty, and power struggles driving the plot.

Review

Jaat is clearly designed as a star vehicle for Sunny Deol, letting him channel his signature 90s-style bravado. Loud one-liners, heavy action, and larger-than-life masculinity dominate the film. While it successfully rekindles nostalgia, it occasionally feels stuck in its own formula, failing to innovate beyond the tried-and-tested tropes of revenge sagas.

The first half stands out with palpable tension, gripping confrontations, and a non-linear narrative that keeps the audience invested. Randeep Hooda’s portrayal of Ranatunga, a Ravan-obsessed villain, adds an interesting layer, providing one of the few inventive moments in the film. Sunny Deol’s performance, full of thunderous dialogues, is undeniably the film’s heart, while Vineet Kumar Singh’s portrayal of the ruthless Somulu makes for a formidable antagonist.

Visually, the film impresses with gritty cinematography and Thaman S’s evocative background score. Action sequences are slick and stylized, and the dialogues, penned by Saurabh Gupta and Sai Madhav Burra, are theatrical and punchy, often more memorable than the scenes themselves.

However, the second half drags significantly. At 153 minutes, the pacing issues become evident, with unnecessary sequences like Urvashi Rautela’s item number adding to the bloat. Female characters, including Regina Cassandra and Saiyami Kher, are underutilized, with Kher reduced to a mere plot device despite her strong introduction.

Verdict

Jaat is a nostalgia-fueled action drama that caters mainly to fans of Sunny Deol’s classic style. It delivers loud entertainment, impressive action, and memorable dialogues, but lacks narrative depth and innovation. For those seeking old-school heroics, it’s a satisfying watch, though others may find it formulaic.
